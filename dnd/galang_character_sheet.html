Identity

Name: Galang ("bright" / "illuminate")

Age: 23 • Height: ~5'7" • Heritage: Sasak (Lombok)

Family: Rangda-aligned practitioners of ilmu selak. Left three years ago.

Current location: Ruman. Arrived a few weeks ago.

Personality Traits

Quiet and watchful. Speaks carefully. Listens more than he talks.

Uncomfortable with trust — both giving and receiving it. Kindness from strangers makes him wary, not grateful.

Has a dry, dark sense of humor that surfaces unexpectedly, usually at the worst possible moment.

Physically restless at night. Sleeps badly. Walks the perimeter of any camp. The selak transformation sits closer to the surface after dark.

Ideals

Redemption. The powers are what they are. What matters is the hand that holds them. He can't undo Tanjung Luar, but he can make the next choice different.

Self-determination. No one else gets to decide what he becomes. Not his family, not the Rangda, not the darkness inside him. The one Rangda principle he still believes in — just applied differently.

Bonds

Wira. A fisherman's son from Tanjung Luar, now living with a curse Galang's family put on him. Galang doesn't know if Wira is still alive. He is afraid to find out.

His grandmother. Sent away for trying to teach him the sentulak counter-arts. Somewhere in the Islands of Suria, possibly. Finding her is a quiet, persistent pull.

His mother's name. He doesn't say it out loud. The name Galang was her last gift, and he carries it like a lantern he can't bring himself to light.

Flaws

He still flinches from the word "monster." It lands too close to something he's afraid might be true — not because of what he is, but because of what he's done.

He is better at the dark arts than the light, and he knows it, and the ease of it frightens him. The selak form comes naturally. The sentulak counter-arts feel like a language he learned too late.

He runs. When things get complicated — emotionally, socially — his first instinct is to leave. The road is simpler than people.

Physical Description

Lean, weathered, early twenties. Angular face, high cheekbones, broken-and-healed crooked nose. Watchful dark eyes under a heavy brow. Thick black hair roughly self-cut past his ears. Scarred hands and forearms — ritual work, not combat. A deliberate scar along his left collarbone he doesn't talk about.

Dresses plainly: sun-faded cotton shirt, wrapped sarong over trousers, scale mail when armored, dull and unornamented. Smells faintly of crushed herbs and something earthier beneath. At night or in dim light, a faint greenish phosphorescence on his forehead — subtle in torchlight, unmistakable in true darkness.

DM Hooks

Wira's fate. Alive? Curse progressed? Could Galang encounter him — or hear news?

The grandmother. Somewhere in the Islands of Suria. Knows the sentulak tradition properly. Possible personal quest tied to L6 feature (Sentulak — The Returning).

The family / Rangda cell. Involved in the dark magic around Ruman? Coincidence, or did he walk toward the very thing he walked away from?

The green light. Anyone from the selak tradition can recognize it on his forehead. Mark, resource, or target.

Barong Court interest. A Rangda-trained practitioner who left voluntarily and wants to do good would be very interesting to them — and he would find their interest suffocating.

The Name

His mother named him Galang — "bright," "illuminate" — and his father said nothing against it, which was the closest thing to tenderness anyone in the family ever offered. Whether she meant it as a prayer or an apology, Galang has never been sure. She was dead within the year, taken by a fever that the village healer could not touch because the village healer would not come to their house. No one came to their house unless they had to.

The family had a reputation. In the villages scattered across the Islands of Suria, where the rice paddies run right up to the edge of the jungle and the nights are so dark you can lose your hand in front of your face, people knew the name. They didn't speak it after dark.

The Birth

When Galang was born, the orang pintar who attended the birth — a weathered woman with turmeric-stained fingers and eyes that looked through things rather than at them — held him for a long time. Too long. His father told the story later as a point of pride: how the seer's hands had trembled, how she had looked at the family and said nothing, which said everything. The child had ilmu. Strong ilmu. The kind that runs in bloodlines and deepens with each generation.

His father sent word to the Rangda Tribe that same week. The family had been Rangda for as long as anyone could remember — not leadership, not inner circle, but loyal practitioners who believed in what the Rangda stood for. Freedom. The right of those with ilmu to live without the Barong Court's rules, without their registries and their oversight and their polite insistence that power must serve the common good. The Rangda said: your power is yours. No one tells you how to use it. No one tells you when to stop.

It was a good philosophy, in the abstract. Galang's family took it further than the abstract.

The Family

They called themselves practitioners of ilmu selak — the transforming art. The power to shed your human shape and take on something older, something with teeth and hunger and the cold patience of a predator. In the family's telling, this was a gift: a covenant with forces that predated the gods, a source of strength that the soft people in their soft villages could never understand. The Rangda's philosophy of freedom gave the family permission to practice without limit. No oversight. No restraint. No one asking uncomfortable questions about what the selak tradition demanded of its students.

Ilmu hitam. Dark knowledge. The art of becoming something other than human.

Not all Rangda practiced like this. Galang understood that even as a child. The Rangda Tribe was vast and varied — philosophers, rebels, hermits, freedom fighters, people who simply wanted to be left alone. The family occupied one edge of that spectrum: the edge where "freedom from control" became "freedom from consequence." Where the power to transform was also the power to intimidate, to enforce, to remind nearby communities what happened when you crossed people who could become something with teeth.

He participated. That is the part he cannot escape, no matter how far he walks.

As a child, he thought it was normal. Every family had its secrets, its rituals, its things-done-after-dark. He learned to draw the circles. He learned the chants that tasted like copper on his tongue. He learned to hold still while the green light crept across his forehead for the first time — eight years old, shaking, watching his fingers lengthen and his nails curve and split, feeling his spine reshape itself into something that wanted to crouch, to hunt, to feed — and his father's hand on his shoulder, steady, proud, saying good, good, hold the shape, don't fight it.

He held the shape. He didn't fight it. For years, he didn't fight it.

The Slow Rot

The discomfort came on gradually, like a tooth going bad. You don't notice it until one day the pain is all you can think about.

He was fifteen when he first refused an assignment — the family had been asked to deliver a warning to a fishing village whose elders had been talking to Barong Court recruiters. Nothing dramatic — he simply didn't go. His father beat him, and he went the next time, and the time after that. But the refusal had happened, and it sat inside him like a seed.

By seventeen, the seed had roots. He started asking questions that no one in the family wanted to answer. Why this village? What did they actually do? Who decides who gets warned and who gets worse? His father stopped answering. His uncle stopped speaking to him entirely. The family's work continued, and Galang's hands were in it, and every night the green light came easier and the shapes came faster and the thing inside him that was not human grew more comfortable in his skin — and every morning he liked himself a little less.

He started to notice things he'd trained himself not to see. The way villagers flinched when the family passed. The way mothers pulled their children inside. The way the Rangda cell his family answered to never did its own enforcement — that's what families like Galang's were for. The freedom the Rangda promised was real, but it flowed upward. The people at the top were free. The people at the bottom — the normal people, the ones without ilmu, the ones who just wanted to fish and farm and raise their children — they were free to be afraid.

Wira

His name was Wira.

A fisherman's son from a village called Tanjung Luar, a three-hour walk through the paddies from Galang's family compound. They'd met as boys — one of those friendships that happens when two kids are both at the river at the same time and one of them has a fishing line and the other doesn't. Wira had no ilmu. He was completely, unremarkably ordinary: a skinny kid with a loud laugh who was good with knots and bad with directions, who wanted to build boats when he grew up, who didn't know what Galang's family did and never thought to ask.

For years, Wira was the only person in Galang's life who looked at him and saw a person rather than a practitioner. Galang kept the friendship secret — not because it was forbidden, but because he understood, with the instinct of someone raised in a house full of predators, that anything he visibly cared about could be used against him. He visited when he could. They fished. They talked about nothing. It was the only part of his life that didn't smell like copper and old blood.

Tanjung Luar's elders made a mistake. It wasn't even a large mistake — they accepted a visit from a Barong Court envoy, listened politely, and declined to formally ally. But they declined publicly, which meant the Rangda cell heard about it, which meant someone decided that a message needed to be sent. Not to the elders. To the village. The kind of message that reminds an entire community what it means to entertain the wrong visitors.

Galang's father volunteered the family. He brought Galang.

What happened in Tanjung Luar took less than an hour. The family came at night — of course at night, the selak tradition is always at night — and they did what they were sent to do. Green light in the paddies. Shapes moving between the houses. The sound of people screaming who had never had cause to scream before. No one was supposed to die. That was the understanding: a demonstration, not a massacre. Fear, not death.

Wira's father tried to protect his home with a fishing gaff. He was not a brave man — he was a terrified man doing what a terrified father does. One of Galang's cousins broke his arm. When Wira ran out to help, he collided with Galang in the dark — literally ran into him, face-to-face, close enough to see the green light and the shape beneath it and the human eyes still inside the thing that was no longer entirely human. Wira recognized him. Galang saw the recognition happen. And then one of the cousins, not knowing, not caring, lashed out at the boy who was running toward the family.

Wira didn't die. That would have been simpler. What the selak curse did to him was worse in some ways — a lingering affliction that settled into his body like rot into wood. The village orang pintar couldn't remove it. Galang heard, weeks later, that Wira couldn't use his right hand anymore. That he woke screaming. That he didn't laugh.

Galang's father called it a clean job. Minimal damage. Message delivered.

Galang packed a bag that night. He walked out through the rice paddies while the frogs screamed in the ditches. He did not look back. He has not been back.

The Road

He has been walking for three years.

The Wayfarer's road is not a philosophy for Galang — it is a practical reality. He has no home, no community, no network of allies. The villages he passes through sense something wrong about him even when the green light isn't showing: a faint smell of decay that clings to his clothes, a way of moving that is too quiet, too watchful, too much like something hunting. Children stare. Dogs whine and back away. Old women make signs against evil and look the other way.

He has tried to learn the counter-arts — the sentulak, the "returning" — from fragments and whispers, from half-remembered lessons his grandmother once muttered about before his father silenced her. The old woman had known the ilmu putih, the white knowledge, the defensive tradition that exists specifically to check and balance the dark. She tried to teach him, quietly, in the gaps between his father's lessons. His father found out and put a stop to it — not violently, but absolutely. The grandmother was moved to a relative's house on another island. Galang never saw her again.

He has pieced together what he can of the sentulak from those fragments. He is not good at it yet. He is better at the dark arts than the light, and that fact sits in his chest like a stone.

The Rangda have not come for him. He doesn't know why. Maybe his father covered for him — some residual shred of the feeling that was almost tenderness. Maybe the cell considers him too minor to pursue. Maybe they are patient, and the pursuit is simply slow. He doesn't know. The not-knowing is its own kind of weight.

Ruman

He arrived in Ruman a few weeks ago, drawn by nothing more specific than the road ending at the coast and a ferry that was leaving. The Islands of Suria are neutral ground — open to outsiders, aligned with neither Barong nor Rangda — and Ruman is the kind of place where a stranger can exist without being examined too closely. The town smells of incense and salt. People do what they do because they believe it should be done. No one asks why. No one asks him why, either, which suits him.

He sleeps in different places — under a boat hull near the docks, in the treeline behind the temple complex, once in a rice storage hut whose owner either didn't notice or didn't care. He has no routine yet, no stall where they know his face, no one who nods when he passes. He is a stranger who smells faintly wrong, and Ruman is polite enough not to mention it.

He has heard the whispers. Something dark moving in the towns and villages around Ruman. Refugees arriving with curses on their skin and panic in their eyes. Creatures born from dark magic. The kind of infestation that smells, to Galang, uncomfortably like home.

He knows what dark magic looks like from the inside. He knows what it does to communities, to families, to boys who just wanted to build boats. He knows that someone with ilmu should do something about it, and he knows that he has ilmu, and he knows that "not doing anything is just waiting for whatever problem is out there to come here."

He does not want to be a hero. He does not trust himself to be one. But he has dark powers and a debt he cannot name, and the screaming has started again in the nearby villages, and he is done walking away from it.
